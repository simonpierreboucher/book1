2  Time Value of Money

2.1 Future Value (Capitalization)

In this section, we will look at how to capitalize a single amount. Capitalization allows us to determine the future value of an amount of money invested today at a specified rate. Here are the parameters we will use:

  • \(P_0=\) Amount invested in \(t=0\)
  • \(r=\) Periodic interest rate received on the investment
  • \(n=\) Period during which the initial amount is invested
  • \(P_n=\) Future value of our investment \(P_0\) in \(t=n\)

Here is the equation allowing us to find the future value of our investment: \[ P_n=P_0 \times (1+r)^n \]

2.2 Present Value (Discounting)

In this section, we’ll look at how to discount a single amount. Discounting allows us to determine the present value of an amount of money that we will receive in the future. Here are the parameters we will use:

  • \(P_0=\) Amount to be received in the future in today’s value (\(t = 0\)), i.e. the present value
  • \(r=\) Periodic interest rate in effect in the economy
  • \(n=\) Number of periods before the amount is received.
  • \(P_n=\) Nominal amount to be received in the future (\(t=n\))

Here is the equation for us to find the present value of an amount to be received in the future.

\[ P_0=\frac{P_n}{(1+r)^n} \]

2.3 Annuity

An annuity is simply a series of identical payments received at constant intervals during a specified period.

  • \(A\)$ represents the payment received in each period
  • \(n\) represents the number of payments the annuity will pay

2.3.1 Future value of a regular annuity

The future value of a regular annuity, will give us the value of the sum of all flows \(A\$\) capitalized at \(t = n\). \(VF_{P_n}\) will be the notation we will use for the future value of a regular annuity. The following equation shows how to get the future value of our regular annuity.

\[ VF_{P_n} = A \times (1+r)^{n-1}+A \times (1+r)^{n-2}+....+A \times (1+r)^{1}+A \times (1+r)^{0} \]

If the number of periods \(n\) is relatively small, it will be easy to calculate the future value. However, if we have a large number of \(n\) periods, the following formula is more convenient.

\[ VF_{P_n}=A \left[\frac{(1+r)^n-1}{r} \right] \]

Knowing the following parameters:

  • \(VF_{P_n}=\) Future value of a regular annuity
  • \(A=\) constant cash flow received in each period
  • \(n=\) Number of cash flow that will be paid in this regular annuity
  • \(r=\) current periodic interest rate

2.3.2 Present value of a regular annuity

The present value of a regular annuity will give us the value of the sum of all the flows \(A\$\) discounted in \(t = 0\). \(VP_{P_0}\) will be the notation we will use for the present value of a regular annuity. The following equation shows how to get the present value of our regular annuity.

\[ VP_{P_0} = \frac{A}{(1+r)^{1}}+\frac{A}{(1+r)^{2}}+....+\frac{A}{(1+r)^{n-1}}+\frac{A}{(1+r)^{n}} \]

If the number of periods \(n\) is relatively small, it will be easy to calculate the present value. However, if we have a large number of \(n\) periods, the following formula is more convenient.

\[ VP_{P_0}=A \left[ \frac{1-\frac{1}{(1+r)^n}}{r} \right] \]

Knowing the following parameters:

  • \(VP_{P_0}=\) Present value of a regular annuity
  • \(A=\) constant cash flow received in each period
  • \(n=\) Number of cash flow that will be paid in this regular annuity
  • \(r=\) current periodic interest rate
